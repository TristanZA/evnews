Why SA Imports Battery Cells

Tristan Wiggill March 31, 2026 minerals

South Africa is the world's number one producer of manganese, outputting around 7.4 million tonnes in 2024. This is roughly the same amount as Gabon and Australia combined. 

The country also holds significant vanadium reserves, with Bushveld Vanadium operating one of the most advanced vanadium electrolyte production facilities in the world. 

Furthermore, the broader Southern African Development Community (SADC) region contains virtually every mineral needed to manufacture a modern battery cell: lithium (Zimbabwe), cobalt (DRC), copper (Zambia), graphite (Mozambique), nickel, iron, phosphorus, and aluminium.

Yet, according to the Faraday Institution's landmark "Charging Ahead" report (March 2026), South Africa does not manufacture a single Li-ion battery cell locally. Every cell in every electric vehicle (EV), every solar backup system, and every grid-scale storage installation is imported, overwhelmingly from China.

This is the Mineral Paradox.

The Numbers Tell the Story

Of all the manganese South Africa produces, only approximately 5% is used domestically, processed by the Manganese Metal Company (MMC) at its Mbombela refinery, which has been operational since 1974. 

The remaining 95% is exported as raw ore or basic metal, with the value-added processing — and the jobs and revenue that come with it — happening elsewhere. The situation with vanadium is similar. Bushveld Vanadium exports the majority of its production, with the Netherlands being the primary destination. 

Between 2019 and 2023, vanadium exports to the Netherlands grew by approximately 56%. South Africa ships the raw material; Europe captures the value. Meanwhile, South Africa's Li-ion battery import bill has grown dramatically. 

Import data from Trade Map shows that China dominates the supply of lithium cells and battery packs into South Africa, with the value of imports rising sharply from 2019 to 2023 as demand for solar storage and EVs accelerated.

Why Hasn't South Africa Closed the Gap?

The report identifies several structural barriers:

Refining capacity is the missing link. Mining the ore is one thing; processing it into battery-grade chemicals (manganese sulfate, vanadium pentoxide, lithium hydroxide) is an entirely different industrial capability. South Africa currently lacks the refining infrastructure to convert its raw mineral wealth into the high-purity precursor materials that cell manufacturers require.

Cell manufacturing requires massive scale. China produces approximately 85% of the world's battery cells and storage systems. The capital investment, supply chain depth, and economies of scale required to compete are enormous. South Africa cannot simply "build a factory" and expect to be competitive overnight.

Policy has not yet aligned with opportunity. Unlike Zimbabwe, which in 2022 banned the export of unprocessed lithium ore to force local beneficiation, South Africa has not yet implemented equivalent "beneficiation mandates" for manganese or vanadium. The minerals leave the country before the value is added.

The skills pipeline is thin at the top. While South Africa has strong graduate-level mining and engineering talent, the report notes a "crippling shortage" of Masters and PhD-level specialists in battery chemistry, materials science, and electrochemical engineering — the exact skills needed to design and operate a cell manufacturing facility.

The Green Shoots: What Is Happening

The picture is not entirely bleak. The report highlights several encouraging developments that suggest the gap is beginning to close:

MMC's new plant: The Manganese Metal Company is currently constructing a 6,000 tonne-per-annum Phase 1 plant to manufacture high-purity manganese sulfate monohydrate — a direct battery precursor material. This is a significant step from raw ore to battery-grade chemical.

Bushveld's electrolyte facility: Bushveld Vanadium's electrolyte production plant in East London has an initial target capacity of 8 million litres per year, with potential expansion to 32 million litres. This positions South Africa as a potential global supplier of vanadium electrolyte for flow batteries.

Local pack assembly is growing. While cell manufacturing remains absent, approximately 10 South African companies — including iG3N, Blue Nova, Freedom Won, Solar MD, and REVOV — are assembling Li-ion battery packs for stationary storage and EV applications. This is midstream activity, not upstream cell production, but it represents a growing local capability.

Mintek's pilot programme: The state-owned research organisation Mintek is running a pilot-scale demonstration of recovery and production of battery-grade salts of nickel, manganese, and copper, as well as battery-grade purified phosphoric acid — the building blocks of a future local cell manufacturing industry.

What the 2026 Roadmap Says

The Charging Ahead report's roadmap for South Africa is explicit: local cell manufacturing must be a long-term national strategic goal, not an afterthought. 

The short-term priority (2025–2030) is to develop battery-grade chemicals and precursors — the refining step that currently sends value offshore. The mid-term goal (2030–2035) is to build at least 10 GWh of local Li-ion cell manufacturing capacity.

For context, 10 GWh is modest by global standards — Tesla's Nevada Gigafactory produces over 35 GWh per year — but it would be transformative for South Africa's industrial base and could create up to 60,000 new jobs across the battery value chain.

The report also notes a strategic decision that South Africa must make in collaboration with its SADC neighbours: which minerals to refine locally, and which to refine at source. 

For manganese, aluminium, and nickel, local refining already makes economic sense. For lithium (from Zimbabwe), cobalt (from the DRC), and graphite (from Mozambique and Madagascar), refining at the source country may be more viable — or South Africa could explore synthetic graphite production from its abundant coal reserves.

What This All Means 

For South African businesses investing in EVs and charging infrastructure today, the Mineral Paradox has a direct practical implication: you are entirely dependent on imported battery technology, with all the currency risk, supply chain vulnerability, and price volatility that entails.

The good news is that this is a solvable problem — and South Africa has more of the raw ingredients to solve it than almost any other country on earth. 

The question is whether policy, industrial investment, and skills development can align fast enough to capture the opportunity before it moves elsewhere.
