Cape Town to get SA's first locally built electric buses

Tristan Wiggill April 17, 2026 public transport

Cape Town is preparing to take delivery of its first electric bus in August, the start of a phased rollout that will bring 30 new vehicles into the MyCiTi fleet by mid-2027.

The 12-metre Volvo BZRLE buses will be the first electric buses in South Africa to have their bodies manufactured locally. The bodywork will be produced in Johannesburg, giving the project an added dimension as a boost to domestic industry.

The City of Cape Town's Urban Mobility Directorate will receive the first bus in August, followed by 13 more before the end of the year. The remaining vehicles will arrive by June 2027.

Subject to a successful testing period, passenger services are expected to begin on 1 July 2027, on routes linking Mitchells Plain and Khayelitsha with Wynberg, Claremont and the Cape Town CBD.

The new buses will serve MyCiTi's metro south-east network, including routes forming part of the Phase 2A expansion. Two new depots built to house the fleet were completed in October last year at a cost of R430 million.

Each facility spans 7.5 hectares and includes underground charging infrastructure, mechanical workshops and capacity for up to 250 buses as the fleet grows. Rob Quintas, the mayoral committee member for urban mobility, said the timing of the rollout was well suited to the current environment.

"The delivery of the electric bus fleet cannot be more timeous, given the volatility of the energy markets and uncertain geopolitical environment," he said. "Apart from lowering our carbon emissions, an electric bus fleet could offer multiple other benefits, especially as far as maintenance and operational costs are concerned."

International experience points to operating cost reductions of up to 70% for electric bus fleets, though the city will conduct its own research to determine what savings are achievable locally. Before the buses enter regular service, the city will run a 12-month testing programme in partnership with the University of Cape Town.

The research, funded jointly by the city and the Urban Electric Mobility Initiative, will assess battery performance across different route profiles, the effect of passenger loads and weather conditions on energy consumption, and charging times. The findings will inform driver training, maintenance planning and the broader rollout scheduled for 2027.

The city has indicated that acquiring additional electric buses remains a priority. Further expansion will depend on funding from national government. Cape Town's MyCiTi network currently operates 347 buses across 39 routes, carrying around 2.3 million passengers a month.

The electric fleet represents the most significant change to the service since its Phase 2A expansion began, and positions the city as the first in South Africa to operate locally built electric buses at scale.
