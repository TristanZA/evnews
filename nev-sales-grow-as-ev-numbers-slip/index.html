NEV sales grow as EV numbers slip

Tristan Wiggill April 18, 2026 EV Adoption

South Africa's New Energy Vehicle (NEV) market, which includes hybrid and plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs) alongside battery electric vehicles (BEVs), grew 7.1% to 16,716 units in 2025. 

This follows a doubling of NEV sales in 2024, which industry body naamsa | the Automotive Business Council described as "early-stage demand momentum."

BEV sales, however, told a more sobering story. 

Pure electric vehicle registrations declined from 1,257 units in 2024 to 1,088 units in 2025, a retreat that industry participants attributed to a combination of high purchase prices, inadequate charging infrastructure, and punishing import duties.

A Taxing Situation

Tshetlhe Litheko, Chief Policy Officer at naamsa | the Automotive Business Council, noted that South Africa's EV import duty of approximately 25%, compounded by VAT and an ad valorem luxury tax, is in sharp contrast to the 10% levied by the European Union and the 2.5% base rate applied in the United States. 

China, meanwhile, has moved to eliminate import duties on EVs for many trading partners, while simultaneously removing domestic purchase taxes to accelerate adoption.

“South Africa holds a proactive NEV and EV transition agenda, with a 150% investment incentive effective from March 2026," Litheko said, pointing to the NEV roadmap milestone that became operative last month.

Furthermore, the South African Automotive Masterplan (SAAM 2035) wants 60% of new vehicle sales in SA by 2035 to be NEVs.

Infrastructure and affordability the central challenges

Despite policy progress, the gap between aspiration and reality remains wide. 

South Africa has fewer than 400 public charging stations compared with approximately 4,800 petrol stations. Grid instability further complicates reliable charging access, though solar-powered charging solutions are beginning to emerge as a practical workaround.

Affordability, though, remains the sharpest constraint. 

Most EVs on the South African market are priced above R600 000, yet 74% of prospective buyers are targeting vehicles below R400 000. That mismatch will require both policy intervention and a broader range of affordable EV models to resolve.

The aftermarket opportunity

“The aftermarket opportunity in EVs is substantial,” says Anton Fiets, Executive Manager for Industry Development at the Automotive Industry Development Centre (AIDC). 

The AIDC's Manufacturing Centre of Excellence, currently in its first phase, incorporates EV simulation and high-voltage training systems alongside mechatronics and advanced assembly workstations.

Fiets points to battery diagnostics, replacement and recycling services, software and telematics support, and specialist EV parts supply as areas where South African businesses could build competitive positions, drawing on lessons from China, Europe, and the United States.

Manufacturing and logistics pressure increases

Ongoing conflict in the Middle East has pushed oil above $110 per barrel, raising manufacturing costs and logistics pressures. 

US tariffs, rapid technological change, and intensifying competition from Asian EV imports are reshaping the OEM landscape, while the growth of wholly imported completely knocked-down (WICKD) models threatens to reduce localisation levels.

KZN's business confidence reached a historic high of 60 points in the fourth quarter of 2025, according to the Bureau for Economic Research, making it South Africa's most confident business hub. 

Whether that confidence translates into the investment needed to accelerate the NEV transition locally will depend, in large part, on whether government and industry can close the gap between policy ambition and on-the-ground infrastructure reality.
