Electric Mobility A Shield Against Global Volatility

Tristan Wiggill April 3, 2026 electric mobility

In the 1970s South Africans faced strict fuel rationing, 80km/h speed limits, and dry weekends where not a drop of petrol could be bought. 

Conflict in the Middle-East back then was a stark lesson in how vulnerable a nation becomes when its transport and logistics are tethered to a foreign fossil fuel supply. Fast forward to 2026, and the geopolitical landscape feels eerily familiar. 

As tensions flare again in the Middle East, the global energy market is once more leaning into unpredictability. The fundamental difference is that today, viable alternatives exist.

The Fragility of the Status Quo

As a net energy importer, South Africa remains dangerously exposed. The country currently imports nearly 80% of its oil. 

When international markets shift, inflation spikes, transport costs soar, and the price of everyday goods rises because the trucks moving them are burning expensive, imported fuel.

The country is arguably more dependent on energy today than it was fifty years ago, and the traditional fuel sources underpinning that dependence are subject to forces entirely beyond its control.

The 18-Gigawatt Silver Lining

Whilst the global oil market remains a wildcard, South Africa's domestic energy landscape is quietly undergoing a revolution. 

By the end of 2025, private sector renewable energy projects had reached a significant 18-gigawatt milestone, with solar accounting for over 12.5 gigawatts of that capacity.

This is fundamentally about energy sovereignty. Every kilowatt-hour generated by a South African solar panel to power an electric vehicle is a rand that remains within our borders rather than flowing abroad to pay for foreign oil. 

The capacity to fuel our own vehicles with domestically generated electricity is well within reach.

Learning from the First Movers

Bold blueprints already exist elsewhere. 

In early 2024, Ethiopia became the first country in the world to ban the importation of fossil fuel-powered vehicles, motivated by the steady drain on its foreign currency reserves. 

By pivoting to domestically sourced electricity, Ethiopia is securing its energy future whilst nurturing a local EV manufacturing base.

In South Africa, the maturity of the EV market and an expanding public charging infrastructure signal that the experimental phase is behind us. Consumers and businesses are already realising the benefits of lower maintenance and operating costs, along with greater long-term financial predictability.

Furthermore, there is a strong argument that the case for EVs runs deeper than fuel price.

The Path Forward

The ongoing conflict in the Middle East represents a systemic risk to supply chains and economic growth across the globe. South Africa's exposure to international energy disruptions is a structural vulnerability that demands a structural response.

The transition to electric mobility offers that response. 

It provides a sustainable way to move people and goods whilst creating the kind of stable, predictable environment that attracts investor confidence. Rapid adoption is the priority. The goal is to generate our own energy security rather than import the instability of foreign markets.
