The 2026 South Africa Battery Landscape Report

A Comprehensive Guide to the Faraday Institution’s "Charging Ahead" Findings*

South Africa is uniquely placed in the global energy transition. As the continent’s most industrialised economy, the country faces the dual challenge of a fragile national grid and an urgent need to decarbonise its transport and industrial sectors.

The March 2026 Faraday Institution report, "Charging Ahead: Battery Ecosystems, Energy Transitions and Electrification in Emerging Economies," provides a first-of-its-kind roadmap for how South Africa can leverage its vast mineral wealth to become a global leader in battery storage and electric mobility.

The Core Vision: 2025 – 2050

The report outlines a three-phase evolution for the South African battery ecosystem, moving from a raw material exporter to a high-tech manufacturing hub.

Short Term (2025–2030)

Foundation & Strategy

    • Establish clear national strategy for local cell manufacturing

    • Launch collaborative energy planning for grid-scale storage

    • Identify and support local value chain 'quick wins'

    • Initiate large-scale local talent and skills development

Medium Term (2030–2035)

Localisation & Scale

    • Develop sustainable VRFB (Vanadium) market and supply chain

    • Build initial Lithium-ion cell manufacturing capacity

    • Localise vanadium electrolyte and battery production

    • Establish a formal end-of-life battery management ecosystem

Long Term (2035–2050)

Global Competitiveness

    • Achieve global competitiveness in specialised battery niches

    • Drive significant job creation through the full value chain

    • Full integration of batteries into a stable, renewable grid

    • South Africa as a net exporter of battery technology

Key Findings for South Africa

Mineral Advantage: South Africa holds the world's largest reserves of manganese and vanadium—two critical components for modern battery chemistries. While the country currently exports these as raw ores, the report identifies a significant economic opportunity in local refining and precursor production (see Why SA Imports Battery Cells).

Energy Security Link: Unlike many developed markets where batteries are a "nice-to-have" for grid optimisation, in South Africa, Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are a "must-have" for survival. The report highlights how storage is the only viable way to manage load shedding while integrating new wind and solar capacity.

E-Mobility Transition: While passenger EV adoption is growing, the report suggests that the real "tipping point" for South Africa lies in commercial fleets and public transport, where the total cost of ownership (TCO) benefits are most pronounced.

Skills Gap: A recurring theme is the urgent need for specialised technical skills. From battery chemistry research to high-voltage safety for fleet technicians, South Africa must invest in its workforce to avoid becoming a mere consumer of foreign technology.

*Disclaimer: The findings summarised here are based on the South Africa chapter of the Faraday Institution’s "Charging Ahead" report (March 2026). 

Country-specific sections of this report reflect the perspectives and research of the individual "Battery Ambassadors" appointed for each region. 

While providing a valuable landscape assessment, these findings have not been independently verified by the Faraday Institution or the UK Government, and should be viewed as a strategic guide rather than a definitive policy document.
