SA’s EV Industry Has a Carbon Problem

Tristan Wiggill April 16, 2026 Industry Reports

South Africa's electric vehicle sector is growing. New models are arriving, charging networks are expanding, and government is slowly warming to the idea of a cleaner transport future. 

But there is a problem that very few people in the industry are talking about openly. It has nothing to do with range anxiety or charging infrastructure. It has to do with the electricity that powers the factories making these vehicles, and a European trade rule that is already in force.

What is CBAM?

The EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — known as CBAM — is a carbon tariff on goods imported into Europe. 

It targets products made using carbon-intensive energy. The idea is straightforward. If a factory in South Africa uses dirty, coal-fired electricity to manufacture goods, and those goods are then sold into the European Union, the buyer in Europe pays a carbon levy on top of the normal import price.

CBAM entered its transitional phase in October 2023. Full enforcement began in January 2026. 

Why this matters for South Africa's EV sector

South Africa's automotive industry is one of the country's most important export sectors. It supplies vehicles and components to markets around the world, including Europe. 

As the industry shifts towards electric vehicles, both in terms of what it assembles locally and what it exports, the carbon intensity of the electricity used in production becomes a direct business cost.

South Africa generates roughly 80% of its electricity from coal. That makes it one of the most carbon-intensive grids in the world. A vehicle or component assembled here, using grid electricity, carries a significant carbon footprint before it even leaves the factory floor.

Under CBAM, European importers of those goods will need to account for that carbon. They will pay for it. And in a competitive global market, that cost will eventually find its way back to South African manufacturers in the form of lower margins, lost contracts, or pressure to clean up their supply chains.

The electricity reform connection

This is where South Africa's ongoing electricity reform becomes directly relevant to the EV industry, and why a research report published in early 2026 by the South Africa Electricity Traders Association (SAETA) and research firm Krutham deserves attention from anyone working in this space.

The report, titled Policy to Power, makes the case that electricity market reform is not solely about keeping the lights on. It is also about decarbonisation, competitiveness, and reducing South Africa's exposure to exactly these kinds of carbon trade measures.

The report states clearly that market reform "underpins decarbonisation by enabling rapid investment in renewables, supporting corporate decarbonisation and reducing exposure to carbon border measures such as the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism."

In other words, the faster South Africa reforms its electricity market and opens it up to renewable energy, the faster manufacturers can access cleaner power, and the lower their CBAM exposure becomes.

The wheeling opportunity

One of the most practical tools available right now is electricity wheeling. This allows a business to buy renewable energy directly from an independent power producer and have it delivered across the national grid to their facility. 

Electricity traders, a relatively new category of licensed market participant in South Africa, play a central role here. They aggregate supply from multiple renewable generators and match it with business customers, including manufacturers. 

The SAETA report notes that traders already have a pipeline of 18 gigawatts of renewable energy projects in development. That is a significant pool of clean power waiting to reach businesses that need it.

For an EV manufacturer or component supplier looking to reduce its carbon footprint ahead of European export deadlines, this is not a theoretical option. It is available today, in metros and areas where wheeling frameworks are in place.

The catch

The problem is that access to wheeling is uneven. 

Only 10 of South Africa's 257 municipalities have adopted wheeling policies. That means a factory located in the wrong municipality simply cannot access green power through this route, regardless of how much renewable energy is available on the grid.

The SAETA report identifies this as one of the key blockages holding back electricity reform. Municipal distribution reform is listed as one of its ten priority actions. Until more municipalities open their networks to wheeling, the CBAM risk for manufacturers in those areas remains unaddressed.

What energy-intensive EV firms should be doing

The CBAM is not going away. If anything, the EU is likely to expand its scope over time. For South African businesses exporting to Europe, or hoping to, the core issue is how quickly they can adapt.

A few practical starting points:

  • Understand your carbon exposure. Map the electricity consumption in your production process and calculate the carbon intensity based on the South African grid average. This is the number that will determine your CBAM liability.
  • Explore wheeling options. If your facility is in a municipality with a wheeling policy, speak to a licensed electricity trader about accessing renewable power. The cost savings and carbon benefits can be significant.
  • Engage with the reform process. Industry bodies, including those representing the automotive and EV sectors, should be actively engaging with NERSA and the Department of Electricity and Energy on trading rules, wheeling frameworks, and the pace of electricity market reform. 

The bigger picture

South Africa has a narrow window to get this right. 

The SAETA report warns that load shedding could return from 2029 as coal plants retire and demand rises. At the same time, the pressure from CBAM and South Africa's own carbon tax is building. The two forces are converging on the same point: the electricity grid needs to get cleaner, faster.

The risk for the EV industry is that slow reform leaves manufacturers exposed to rising carbon costs on their European exports. The opportunity is that businesses which move early, securing green power through wheeling and traders, will have a genuine competitive advantage over those that wait.

Source: Policy to Power — Ten Actions to Deliver Green, Accessible and Secure Electricity, SAETA/Krutham (2026)
