Petrol, diesel or PHEV: which makes financial sense?

Tristan Wiggill March 31, 2026

Global oil markets have been unsettled by ongoing tensions between Iran and the United States, and the effects are reaching South African forecourts.

The threat to supply routes through the Strait of Hormuz has kept energy traders on edge, and that pressure is now translating into real costs for local motorists.

Forecourt pressure mounts

Brent crude has moved sharply on each new development in the region. Data from the Central Energy Fund has pointed to the risk of a significant fuel-price increase in April, with some grades potentially moving beyond R25 per litre if current conditions persist. Gauteng 95 ULP already sits at R20.30 per litre, leaving motorists with little buffer against further increases.

For most vehicle owners, the monthly repayment is only part of the financial picture. The more pressing question is what a vehicle costs to run week to week, and that is where powertrain choice starts to matter more than it might in a stable fuel-price environment.

Comparing the cost per kilometre

To put some numbers to that question, three family SUVs were compared using their quoted combined fuel-consumption figures and current Gauteng pump prices.1

The vehicles selected were the Toyota RAV4 2.0 GX-R AWD running on petrol, the Hyundai Tucson 2.0D Executive on diesel, and the JAECOO J7 SHS, a plug-in hybrid.

The RAV4 returns a quoted 6.7 litres per 100 km. At R20.19 per litre for 95 ULP, that works out to approximately R135.27 per 100 km. The Tucson is rated at 7.4 litres per 100 km, and at R18.60 per litre for diesel, the cost comes to roughly R137.64 per 100 km.

The two are closer than many buyers might expect, given that diesel has traditionally been seen as the more economical option for higher-mileage use.

The J7 SHS returns a quoted combined figure of 4.7 litres per 100 km, dropping to a manufacturer-claimed 1.0 litre per 100 km when the battery is fully charged.

Using the 4.7-litre figure, the running cost works out to approximately R94.89 per 100 km, which is around R40 less than the RAV4 and R43 less than the Tucson over the same distance.

Scaled up to a Johannesburg to Durban return trip, the JAECOO saves roughly R457 against the Toyota and approximately R484 against the Hyundai on fuel alone.

Why PHEVs are gaining ground

Those figures illustrate why plug-in hybrids are attracting more attention as pump prices rise. They occupy a practical middle ground, offering lower fuel consumption than a conventional internal-combustion vehicle without the dependency on public charging infrastructure that comes with a fully electric car.

For buyers in areas where charging networks remain patchy, that is a meaningful consideration.

The efficiency advantage of a PHEV is, however, conditional. The savings depend on the battery being charged consistently and regularly. When that happens, the fuel-consumption figures become achievable in everyday driving.

When it does not, the vehicle effectively operates as a heavier petrol car, and the consumption figures shift accordingly. Electricity costs also need to be factored into the total running-cost calculation, even if they are generally lower than the equivalent fuel spend.

What the comparison does show is that the gap between petrol and diesel running costs has narrowed considerably at current price levels. The more meaningful gap now sits between conventional powertrains and electrified options.

As fuel prices remain under pressure, that gap is likely to become a more prominent factor in purchasing decisions, particularly for buyers covering significant distances each month.
