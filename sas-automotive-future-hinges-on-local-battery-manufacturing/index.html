SA’s Automotive Future Hinges on Local Battery Manufacturing

Tristan Wiggill March 31, 2026 Manufacturing

A new World Bank report suggests that battery manufacturing and local beneficiation of battery minerals will determine whether South Africa can preserve its automotive base and build new industrial capability.

The country’s vehicle production for export has long depended on Europe and the United Kingdom. Those markets will gradually phase out internal combustion engine vehicles, which means original equipment manufacturers in South Africa will face shrinking demand for traditional models. 

A strategy that focuses only on final vehicle assembly will not protect local jobs. But a strategy that integrates battery manufacturing, mineral processing and electric vehicle components most likely will.

GDP Boost

The report shows that an integrated battery value chain could add up to R43.6 billion to South Africa’s gross domestic product each year and support more than 58 000 direct and indirect jobs. 

These gains come from activities that range from mineral refining to precursor production to battery pack assembly and electric vehicle components. But expansion in these segments depends on policy certainty, industrial incentives and a clear national position on electric vehicle production.

South Africa’s mineral endowment places it in a strong position. About 70 percent of the world’s identified manganese resources sit in the Northern Cape and most global demand for high‑purity manganese sulphate used in battery cathodes will outstrip supply well before 2035. 

Growing global concern around mineral security creates an opening for South Africa to move beyond ore exports. Investment in battery‑grade processing for manganese, nickel and vanadium could shift value addition into local industry. 

Component manufacturers stand to benefit from greater domestic availability of battery materials, and new entrants could emerge around battery housing, cooling systems, wiring, charging components and safety systems.

No Time To Wait

There is, however, a second message running through the report: we haven’t got much time. Countries like Morocco and Thailand have already aligned their industrial and trade incentives with electric vehicle production. Morocco secured a critical minerals agreement with the United States that gives its producers access to benefits under the US Inflation Reduction Act. 

Thailand has restructured incentives to attract global electric vehicle investment and is positioned as the regional base for several international manufacturers. South Africa still holds a competitive position, but others are moving faster.

Government’s Electric Vehicle White Paper sets out more than 30 recommendations to support electric vehicle assembly, charging networks and component manufacturing. The revised Automotive Investment Scheme offers grants and accelerated depreciation to encourage investment in electric vehicle assembly and parts. 

The updated Automotive Production and Development Programme now includes electric vehicles in its structure. These steps help, but adjustments are still needed, particularly in tariff treatment and localisation incentives.

Import tariffs on electric vehicles remain higher than tariffs on internal combustion engine vehicles in some categories, which raises consumer costs and limits early local demand. A modest reduction in these tariffs would allow more affordable models to enter the market and stimulate demand that supports local assembly. 

Charge point operators are ready to expand networks, but need a national framework, faster municipal approvals and clarity on electricity tariffs for public charging.

In The Zone

South Africa’s industrial zones also require attention. While special economic zones are important tools for attracting battery and electric vehicle investment, many operate unevenly. Duty relief, energy access and customs processes differ from zone to zone, which creates uncertainty for investors. 

A focused effort to position a few zones as dedicated battery and electric vehicle clusters could give South Africa a more compelling investment proposition.

Furthermore, modern battery manufacturing requires technicians, artisans, process controllers and engineers with experience in chemistry, power electronics and quality control. While South Africa has a strong education and training network, few accredited programmes focus specifically on battery systems. 

Partnerships between industry, technical colleges and universities could develop curricula and apprenticeships that prepare workers for these new roles. Local training capacity will also help South African companies meet the environmental and safety standards required for exporting electric vehicle components.

Making The Link

A strong message in the report is that industrial opportunity and environmental responsibility are linked. International buyers expect clear environmental, social and governance standards in mineral sourcing and manufacturing. Therefore, refineries and battery plants that use clean power, water‑efficient processes and traceable supply chains will be more competitive. 

South Africa’s planned expansion of renewable energy could also support green industrial zones where manufacturers are able to show low‑carbon credentials to export markets. The country’s automotive industry has the skills, supplier networks and manufacturing expertise to succeed in the electric era. 

What it needs is a firm industrial direction that links upstream mineral wealth to downstream vehicle production. Battery manufacturing must not be seen as a side project. Instead, it must be seen as the anchor of future automotive competitiveness and the lever that ensures South Africa remains part of shifting global supply chains.

A clear national stance on electric vehicles, combined with smart incentives and regional coordination, can lift the sector and support investment at every level of the value chain. 

Ultimately, South Africa requires decisive action in order to protect a cornerstone of its economy and position it for future growth.
