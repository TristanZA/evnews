Off-Grid Ambition Meets the Reality of Fleet Electrification

Tristan Wiggill March 31, 2026 fleet electrification

South Africa's fleet electrification conversation has, for several years, been dominated by questions about vehicle availability and purchase cost. 

Less examined is the infrastructure problem sitting underneath both of those concerns. Even if fleet operators were ready to electrify tomorrow, the national electricity grid almost certainly could not support them.

That view is the founding premise of Zero Carbon Charge, a company established in 2022 by Joubert Roux, a Western Cape entrepreneur with a background in service stations, car dealerships and renewable energy development. 

Roux's argument is straightforward. A single town on a national freight corridor, handling a thousand commercial vehicles per day, would generate a five-megawatt electricity demand from charging alone. The distribution network was not designed for anything close to that. 

His response has been to build a network of fully off-grid charging stations powered entirely by on-site solar generation and battery storage, with backup generators for redundancy. 

The first proof-of-concept site, at Voëlfontein in the Northern Cape, has been running for 14 months. Two commercial sites on the N3 are under construction, with equipment already in Durban Harbour and a go-live date of mid-May. A logistics integrator, ZIMI, has contracted to consume 50% of available energy across both sites from day one.

The N3 project will be the first real test of whether the model works commercially, not just technically.

The Economics Are Tighter Than They Look

Zero Carbon Charge prices its DC fast charging at R9 per kilowatt-hour, fixed annually. Each site costs approximately R16.5 million to build in its first phase, with a further R6 million required to double capacity. Roux says the break-even threshold is four charging sessions per day, with profitability reached at seven.

Those numbers are low enough to raise questions about utilisation risk, particularly given that South Africa's EV fleet remains small. Roux acknowledges the challenge but points to the ZIMI off-take agreement as a model for de-risking early-stage sites through contracted commercial demand. 

The sites are also designed to be modular, allowing solar, battery and charging capacity to be scaled incrementally as usage data accumulates rather than requiring operators to commit to full build-out upfront. The fixed-price energy model is likely to attract attention from fleet operators who have spent years absorbing diesel price volatility. 

Whether that interest translates into contracted off-take agreements at the pace Zero Carbon Charge needs remains to be seen. The company's financial viability depends on replicating the ZIMI structure across a significant portion of its planned 120-site network.

"You can control your input costs, so you can control your output costs," Roux adds. That argument becomes considerably more compelling as diesel prices rise. It becomes less compelling if site utilisation lags behind projections in the early years of the rollout.

Commercial Vehicles Change the Scale Entirely

The passenger vehicle market is not where the infrastructure challenge becomes acute. It is in commercial transport, where a single heavy vehicle consumes the energy equivalent of approximately nine passenger EVs per charge cycle. At that scale, the grid constraint Roux identified four years ago is not a future problem. It is a present one.

Zero Carbon Charge has secured sites capable of supporting 50 to 100-megawatt generation setups for dedicated truck charging hubs, and the company is in early discussions about cross-border corridor electrification, including the Gaborone-Durban route and the broader Trans-Kalahari corridor spanning Mozambique, South Africa, Botswana and Namibia. 

Roux estimates that 638 charging stations would be sufficient to electrify the major freight corridors across the continent. That figure is worth sitting with. It implies a capital requirement running into the tens of billions of rands, across multiple regulatory jurisdictions, with no guarantee of the policy support that would make the economics work at that scale. 

Roux is candid about the regulatory friction the company has already encountered in South Africa, including approval processes that have stretched beyond 1,000 days at some sites, and a proposed SANRAL rest and service facility policy that he argues would effectively tax private infrastructure development along national roads.

"The national roads should be a driver of development," he says. "Not an impediment."

What the Tipping Point Requires

Fleet managers watching this space will be aware that EV adoption in South Africa has so far been concentrated in the passenger vehicle segment, and even there, penetration remains low. The arrival of more affordable vehicles in the R300,000 to R400,000 range, led by Chinese manufacturers including BYD, has begun to shift the calculus. 

Plug-in hybrid sales are growing rapidly, and data from Mercedes-Benz suggests that once a market reaches 5% EV penetration of new vehicle sales, it tends to climb to 15% within three years. South Africa has not reached that threshold yet. Getting there will require both affordable vehicles and reliable charging infrastructure to be present simultaneously. 

Zero Carbon Charge is building one side of that equation. The other side depends on import duty policy, OEM investment decisions and the pace at which fleet operators are willing to commit capital to vehicle transition.

The N3 corridor going live in May will not resolve those questions. It will, however, provide the first substantive evidence of whether off-grid charging infrastructure can operate commercially at the scale the country's fleet electrification ambitions require.

That evidence is long overdue.

Zero Carbon Charge's N3 charging stations are scheduled to go live in mid-May. The company's N1 corridor rollout, connecting Cape Town to Musina, is targeted for completion by the end of the year. -Ed.
