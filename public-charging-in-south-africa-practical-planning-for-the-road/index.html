Public Charging in South Africa: Practical Planning for the Road

Tristan Wiggill April 17, 2026 EV Ownership

Public charging is the part of EV ownership that gets the most attention. It’s the part you see on the news and the part that looks the most familiar.

But using a public charger in South Africa is not quite the same as pulling up to a petrol pump. It requires a different way of thinking.

The networks are growing, and the technology is improving, but it is still a system in progress. If you understand how it works now, you can avoid the frustration that many new owners face.

Charging speed is a maximum, not a promise

One of the first things you will notice is that public chargers are labelled with a speed, like 60kW, 80kW or 150kW. It is easy to assume that if you plug into a 150kW charger, your car will always charge at that speed. In reality, it is more complicated.

The speed you get depends on several factors:

  • Your car’s limit: Every EV has a maximum speed it can accept. If your car is limited to 100kW, it will never charge faster than that, even on a 300kW charger.
  • Battery fullness: Charging is fastest when the battery is low. As the battery fills up, it slows down to protect the cells.
  • Temperature: If the battery is excessively hot or cold, the charging speed will drop.
  • Shared power: Many public units have two plugs. If someone else is charging at the same time, the unit often splits its power between the two of you.

Think of the number on the charger as a ceiling, not a guarantee.

Managing the "App Problem"

In South Africa, public charging is managed by several different companies. Most of them have their own apps, payment systems and membership cards. Currently, there is no single app that covers every charger in the country. This means preparation is key.

Before you set off on a trip, you should:

  • Identify which networks are on your route (such as GridCars, Rubicon or others).
  • Download the relevant apps and register your details.
  • Load some credit into the digital wallets on those apps.

Trying to download an app and enter credit card details while standing at a charger in a remote area is a situation you want to avoid. Five minutes of setup at home saves half an hour of stress on the road.

Why the "Plan B" is essential

South Africa presents unique challenges for any electrical network. Infrastructure can be affected by weather, technical faults, or even simple connectivity issues. A charger that shows as "Available" on a map might be offline when you arrive. Or, someone else might be using it, and they could be there for another 40 minutes.

This is why experienced EV owners in South Africa always have a backup plan.

If you are planning to stop at a specific charger, always know where the next nearest one is. Never arrive at a charger with so little battery that you couldn't reach your backup option if you had to. A 15% to 20% safety margin is a sensible rule for long-distance travel here.

The practicalities of a charging stop

What does a real charging stop look like? Public DC chargers use large, heavy cables that are permanently attached to the unit. You park, plug in, and then use your app or an RFID card to start the session.

While the car charges, you generally leave it. This is why chargers are located at malls, convenience pulls, or farm stalls. You go for a coffee, use the bathroom, or stretch your legs. By the time you are ready to leave, the car usually is too.

Most owners find that they don't "wait" for the car to charge. Instead, the car charges while they take a necessary break from driving.

A note on load shedding and reliability

One of the most common questions in South Africa is what happens during load shedding. Many public charging sites at major malls or highway stops have backup power, such as large generators or solar-and-battery systems. However, not all of them do.

Modern charging apps often show the live status of a charger. If a site is down due to a power cut, it will usually show as offline in the app. Checking the app ten minutes before you arrive can save you a wasted detour.

Improving your route planning

For daily driving in the city, you will likely never need a public charger. For long trips, a little digital help goes a long way.

There are tools and websites that help you plan which chargers to use based on your specific car and the route you are taking. They can even predict how much battery you will have left at each stop.

Using these tools removes the guesswork. You stop being a pioneer and start being someone who is just following a map.

The bottom line on public charging

Public charging in South Africa is good enough for most major routes, but it is not yet perfect. It requires you to be a little more organised than you would be in a petrol car. You need the right apps, a little bit of credit, and a clear idea of where your backup stops are.

Once you have those basics sorted, the "anxiety" of charging mostly disappears. It just becomes another part of the journey.
