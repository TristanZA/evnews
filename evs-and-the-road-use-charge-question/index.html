EVs and the fuel levy question

Tristan Wiggill April 18, 2026 taxation

South Africa's roads are funded largely through the fuel levy — a tax built into the price of every litre of petrol or diesel sold. The more fuel you buy, the more you contribute. It is a simple system, and for decades it has worked reasonably well.

But battery electric vehicles (BEVs) do not use these fuels. They charge from the grid, and in doing so, they avoid the fuel levy entirely. As more South Africans switch to BEVs, the amount collected through the fuel levy will shrink — even though the roads those BEVs drive on still need to be built, maintained and repaired.

This is the funding gap that transport planners and economists are increasingly worried about.

Fuel levy figures tell the story

The general fuel levy has been rising steadily.

From June 2025, it stood at R4.01 per litre for petrol and R3.85 per litre for diesel. By April 2026, those figures had increased further to R4.29 per litre for petrol and R4.16 cents per litre for diesel. The Road Accident Fund levy, which funds compensation for road crash victims, also rose to R2.25 per litre.

A petrol car filling up a 50-litre tank is therefore contributing well over R300 in levies alone, every single time. An EV owner filling the same journey pays none of that.

Why a road-use charge makes sense

The idea behind a road-use charge is straightforward.

Instead of taxing the fuel a vehicle burns, the government would charge drivers based on how far they travel. The more kilometres you drive, the more you pay. It does not matter whether your vehicle runs on petrol, diesel, hydrogen or electricity — if you use the road, you contribute to its upkeep.

This also addresses a quieter problem that already exists. A fuel-efficient petrol car consuming six litres per 100 km pays far less in fuel levy per kilometre than an older car consuming 11 litres per 100 km. Both vehicles cause similar wear on the road surface, yet one contributes significantly less to road funding. A per-kilometre charge would treat them the same.

How it could work in practice

There are a few ways a road-use charge could be collected.

The simplest approach would be an odometer reading taken at licensing or roadworthy time each year*. You declare your kilometres, and a charge is calculated accordingly. It is easy to administer and raises few privacy concerns.

A more sophisticated approach would use telematics or GPS tracking. This would allow charges to vary depending on the type of road used, the time of day, traffic conditions and even the weight of the vehicle. A Western Cape pilot concept has already explored this idea, using route-based pricing to show how detailed such a system could become.

The third option is a flat annual fee added to vehicle licence renewal. This is the easiest to implement, but it treats a driver covering 5 000 km a year the same as one covering 50 000 km — which many would consider unfair.

What this means for EV owners

Lower running costs remain one of the strongest arguments for going electric, and that is unlikely to change. A road-use charge, if introduced, would add a new line to the ownership cost — but it would apply to all vehicles, not just BEVs.

The important thing for BEV owners is to follow the debate closely, keep track of annual mileage, and engage when draft proposals are published. The details — how privacy is protected, how rural drivers are treated, how the system is administered — will matter enormously.

How South Africa funds its roads in an electric future is one that government, industry and the public will need to answer together.

*A few issues are worth noting. Firstly, South Africa currently does not require annual roadworthy testing. Secondly, using odometer readings will likely increase the likelihood of "mileage manipulation", especially on older vehicles - Ed.
