Where South Africans Charge Their EVs

Tristan Wiggill April 15, 2026 EV Ownership

There's a common assumption about EV ownership. Most people imagine pulling up to a public charger, plugging in, and waiting. Like a petrol station, but slower. That picture is not wrong exactly. It just isn't the full story.

In practice, most EV owners in South Africa charge in far quieter, more ordinary moments. And understanding where that happens changes how you think about the whole ownership experience.

Home charging: where it usually starts

For most owners, home is where the majority of charging happens.

The reason is simple. The car is already there. It sits overnight, sometimes for eight hours or more. That time is useful. You don't need to go anywhere. You don't need to wait. You plug in before bed and wake up to a full battery.

This is what makes EV ownership feel easy for people whose home setup supports it. Charging stops being an activity. It becomes something that just happens.

What home charging requires

You don't need anything complicated to start charging at home. A standard wall socket can work, particularly if your daily mileage is low and the car has a long time to charge overnight. It is slow, but it gets the job done in many situations.

A wallbox is a step up. It is a dedicated charging unit, usually mounted on a wall in your garage or parking area. It charges faster than a standard socket and is generally the most practical long-term solution for home use.

A certified electrician needs to install it. Your car may come with one included, or with a voucher towards installation. It is worth checking before you buy.

Questions worth asking about your home setup

Before assuming home charging will work for you, a few things are worth thinking through. Where does your car park at home? Is there a power source nearby? Will a cable reach comfortably? Is the parking area covered or exposed to weather?

If you live in an estate or a complex, there may be rules about what you can install. Some body corporates are straightforward about this. Others require applications, approvals, or specific equipment. It is worth finding out early.

If your home already has solar panels or a battery backup system, home charging can become even more practical. Many owners find they can charge largely from their own solar generation during the day, which reduces both cost and dependence on the grid.

Workplace charging: the underrated option

After home, the workplace is where many owners find their second most reliable charging opportunity. The logic is the same as home charging. The car is already parked. It sits for hours while you work. That time can be used.

Workplace charging tends to be slower, similar to home AC charging, but that is fine for a full day of parking. By the time you leave, the battery is topped up. Not every employer offers this yet.

Some do, either as a staff benefit or through a shared charging bay. If yours does, it is worth factoring into your decision. If yours doesn't, it may be worth raising the conversation.

Destination charging: useful when it fits naturally

Some places you visit regularly may already have chargers available.

Shopping centres are the most common example in South Africa. Several major malls now have charging bays in their parking areas. If you spend an hour or two there each week, that time can add meaningful range.

The same applies to gyms, churches, schools, or anywhere else the car sits for a predictable stretch of time. Destination charging works best when it fits into something you are already doing. It is less useful as a dedicated trip.

Public fast charging: for the road, not the routine

Public DC fast chargers are the ones you see on highways and at fuel stops along major routes. They charge quickly. A stop of 20 to 40 minutes can add a significant amount of range, depending on the car and the charger.

They are genuinely useful for long-distance travel. Joburg to Durban, Cape Town to the Garden Route, or any trip that takes you beyond your comfortable daily range.

They are less suited to everyday use. The cost per kilowatt-hour is generally higher than home charging. The chargers can be occupied. They sometimes require specific apps or payment methods. And in South Africa, reliability is still improving. Public fast charging is a valuable part of the picture. It is just not the foundation.

How your living situation shapes everything

South Africa has a wide range of living situations, and they affect EV ownership in different ways.

A freestanding home with a garage is the most straightforward setup. Home charging is easy to arrange, and the car is protected overnight.

An estate or security complex can work well, but it depends on the rules and the available infrastructure. Some estates have already installed shared charging points. Others are still working through the process.

A flat or apartment is more challenging. If there is no dedicated parking with power access, home charging may not be possible. In that case, workplace charging or nearby destination chargers become more important.

A home with solar adds another layer of flexibility. Charging during the day from your own generation is increasingly common and can make the economics of EV ownership even more attractive.

The pattern that makes ownership easy

When you look at how most South African EV owners charge, a pattern emerges.

Home charging does the heavy lifting. Workplace charging fills in the gaps. Destination charging adds a little extra when it fits naturally. Public fast charging handles the long trips.

That combination, working together, is what makes daily EV ownership feel unremarkable in the best possible way. The car charges while life happens. You stop thinking about it.

What to check before you commit

If you are still considering an EV, it is worth mapping out your own charging picture before you buy.

Think through where the car will park at home, whether your workplace has or could have charging, which destinations you visit regularly, and how often you travel long distances. That map will tell you whether EV ownership is likely to feel easy or whether it will require more planning than you are comfortable with.
