The case for EVs runs deeper than fuel price

Tristan Wiggill March 31, 2026 fuel price

Every time fuel prices rise, the conversation around electric vehicles picks up.

Search trends spike, showroom enquiries increase, and suddenly everyone has an opinion on whether now is the right time to make the switch. It is a familiar cycle. What is different this time, according to Volvo Car South Africa, is that the conversation is maturing.

Grant Locke, Managing Director of Volvo Car South Africa, puts it plainly.

"Fuel price increases may start the conversation, but they are not the full story. When you look at the total cost of ownership, the flexibility, and the advancements in battery technology, electric vehicles begin to make a compelling case on their own."

That case, it turns out, has very little to do with the petrol price board outside your nearest garage.

The Maths Behind Electric Ownership

One of the most under-appreciated aspects of EV ownership is what happens after the purchase.

Electric vehicles carry fewer moving parts than their petrol-powered counterparts, which translates into reduced maintenance requirements. Servicing intervals can stretch to every two years, with associated costs that tend to sit well below what traditional vehicle owners are accustomed to paying.

Battery technology has also come a long way. Most manufacturers now back their batteries with warranties of up to eight years or 160,000 kilometres. For anyone eyeing the pre-owned market, battery health certificates are increasingly standard, offering a clear picture of remaining capacity before you commit.

Then there is the charging question, which is where the economics become genuinely interesting. Locke is direct about this.

"Whether it is overnight charging on lower tariffs or making use of solar power, drivers have far more control over their monthly mobility costs."

The numbers support this. Charging 50kWh via a public DC fast charger can set you back around R400, delivering roughly 250 to 350 kilometres of range.

The same charge at home during off-peak hours could cost closer to R175. For a commuter covering 60 kilometres a day on weekdays, monthly energy costs could settle around R750, based on average consumption figures and current electricity tariffs.

A Broader Economic Argument

Fuel prices do not only affect what you pay at the pump.

South Africa moves more than 80% of its goods by road, which means diesel price increases ripple outward into food costs, retail prices, and household budgets across the board. The forecourt is just the most visible pressure point.

This broader context is shifting how South Africans think about their vehicles. An EV is a hedge against a volatile global oil market, a way to reduce exposure to currency fluctuations, and for those with solar installations, a meaningful step towards genuine energy independence.

Infrastructure is also catching up. Charging networks are expanding, and policy frameworks are gradually aligning with the direction of travel.

Locke sees this shift as something more fundamental than a reaction to short-term price pressures. "As more South Africans begin to understand these dynamics, the shift to electric becomes less about reacting to fuel prices and more about making a smarter long-term mobility choice."

The upfront cost of electric vehicles has historically given buyers pause. That calculation is changing.

Running costs are lower, servicing is simpler, and the technology is more reliable than many assume. For South African drivers willing to look past the initial price tag, the long-term picture is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.
