Home Charging Key For Electric Taxis

Tristan Wiggill April 16, 2026 EV Research

A new study published in Nature Sustainability suggests that electrifying Cape Town's minibus taxi fleet is possible. But only if the right charging infrastructure is put in place.

Minibus taxis are the lifeblood of public transport in South Africa. More than 300,000 of them carry around 11 million passengers every day. In Cape Town alone, taxis account for 67% of all public transport trips, serving roughly 830,000 passengers daily.

Most of these passengers live in historically marginalised communities, far from economic centres, and depend on taxis as their only realistic transport option.

Researchers from Stellenbosch University used tracking data from 671 minibus taxis participating in the Western Cape Government's BlueDot programme to assess whether electric vehicles could realistically replace the current petrol and diesel fleet. 

The BlueDot programme fitted taxis with GPS trackers to monitor driving behaviour and reward safe driving. This gave the researchers an unusually detailed, real-world dataset to work with (covering around 12,000 vehicle-days of operation).

Charging access is the deciding factor

The study found that electrification is technically viable, but the results depend heavily on where and when taxis can charge. When taxis had access to both depot charging and home charging, between 92% and 97% of daily operations were successful. When taxis could only charge at depots, that figure dropped to between 61% and 83%.

Interestingly, fast charging made very little difference. Increasing charger power from 22 kilowatts to 50 kilowatts improved viability by only one to five percentage points. Expanding access to more charging locations had a far greater impact than increasing charging speed.

A problem for the electricity grid

Electrifying the taxi fleet would add significant new demand to the electricity grid. The study found that unmanaged charging tends to create sharp peaks in the early morning and evening, times when the grid is already under strain. 

Home charging, in particular, generates an evening peak around 8pm, which coincides with the surge in residential electricity use from households switching on appliances and water heaters.

The researchers suggest that managed charging strategies could help. Time-of-use tariffs, centralised scheduling, and solar-aligned charging — where taxis charge during the middle of the day when solar generation is highest — could all help to smooth demand and reduce pressure on the grid.

Air quality improves, but carbon savings are limited (for now)

One of the clearest benefits of electric taxis is cleaner air. Electric vehicles produce no exhaust emissions, which means a significant reduction in fine particle pollution (PM2.5). This matters enormously for low-income communities, which are both the most reliant on taxis and the most exposed to poor air quality.

South Africa's carbon picture is more complicated. 

The country's electricity grid is still heavily dependent on coal, which means that electric taxis currently produce roughly the same amount of carbon dioxide as petrol vehicles. Diesel vehicles actually produce less in this comparison. As South Africa's grid becomes cleaner over time, the carbon benefits of electric taxis are expected to grow.

Equity must be at the centre of planning

The study highlights a serious equity concern. Drivers who live in informal settlements or areas with unreliable electricity access are less able to charge at home, which makes electric operation much harder for them.

The researchers recommend that authorities prioritise shared community charging hubs in townships and underserved areas, rather than relying solely on home charging.

The research was conducted by M.J. Booysen, B.G. Pretorius and J.T. Sello of Stellenbosch University and was published in Nature Sustainability in April 2026. The full paper is available at https://doi.org/10.1038/s41893-026-01808-9. Licensed under CC BY-NC-ND 4.0.
